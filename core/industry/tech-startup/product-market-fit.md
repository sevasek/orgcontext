## Definition

Product–market fit (PMF) is the degree to which a product satisfies a strong market demand — the state where a meaningful segment of customers finds the product so valuable they would be genuinely disappointed if it disappeared. PMF is not a single moment but a zone that can be approached, achieved, lost, and regained as products and markets evolve.

PMF has two components that must both be present: a product that works reliably and a market that has a real, urgent problem the product solves. Many startups build technically sound products without genuine PMF; others find an eager market but cannot deliver a product that performs.

Indicators of strong PMF include: high organic growth (word-of-mouth referrals), low churn, users who are visibly upset at the thought of losing access (>40% "very disappointed" threshold from the Sean Ellis survey), and pull from the market rather than a need to push awareness.

---

## When to Apply

- **Pre-launch validation:** When deciding whether to build a product, pivot, or double down on a current approach.
- **Growth investment decisions:** Before significantly scaling sales, marketing, or hiring — PMF must precede growth spend or you will amplify a leaking bucket.
- **Pivot evaluation:** When growth stalls, churn rises, or customer feedback is consistently lukewarm — PMF assessment reveals whether to iterate or fundamentally change direction.
- **Investor and board conversations:** When articulating readiness to scale or explaining why growth has been cautious.
- **New market or segment entry:** When an established product enters a new geography, vertical, or customer segment and must re-validate fit.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- If your best users are not visibly upset at the prospect of losing your product, you do not have PMF yet — no amount of distribution fixes this.
- Retention is the primary leading indicator of PMF: if a cohort's retention curve flattens (does not go to zero), you have a signal worth following.
- Do not scale before PMF. Each hire, dollar of ad spend, or sales rep added before PMF makes the eventual pivot more expensive and painful.
- When feedback from customers and feedback from non-customers diverges sharply, the non-customers are telling you about your ceiling; the customers are telling you about your floor.
- PMF is segment-specific. You may have strong PMF with one customer type and zero PMF with another — avoid averaging them together.
- Qualitative depth beats quantitative breadth at early stage: ten interviews with genuinely delighted users teach more than 500 NPS responses.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Vanity metrics as PMF proxy:** Measuring downloads, signups, or page views without looking at retention, engagement depth, or willingness-to-pay. These metrics can look impressive while PMF is absent.
- **Feature accumulation without fit:** Continuously adding features to attract different segments rather than going deep on a specific problem for a specific customer. This produces a product that is "ok for everyone" and indispensable to no one.
- **PMF by committee:** Designing for the average of all feedback rather than the intensity of a specific segment's need. Real PMF is niche before it is broad.
- **Premature scaling:** Hiring sales teams, running large ad campaigns, or expanding internationally before retention is proven. This amplifies the problem rather than the solution.
- **Confusing traction with fit:** Early customers who are friends, family, or early adopters with high pain tolerance may not represent the mainstream market. Validate that ordinary customers in the target segment also find the product essential.
