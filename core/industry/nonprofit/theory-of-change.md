## Definition

A theory of change (ToC) is an explicit, testable explanation of how and why a set of activities, delivered to a specific population, are expected to produce the desired long-term outcomes. It maps the causal pathway from inputs and activities through outputs, short-term outcomes, intermediate outcomes, and ultimately to long-term impact — making the assumptions behind each link visible and open to scrutiny.

Unlike a logic model (which primarily shows what, for whom, and what outputs are produced), a theory of change asks *why* — what mechanisms, conditions, or changes in behavior, systems, or relationships connect the work to the impact. A strong ToC articulates the causal assumptions, the preconditions that must be true, and the contextual factors that affect whether the logic holds.

In a nonprofit context, the ToC serves as the organization's strategic hypothesis about social change and the organizing logic for program design, evaluation, and resource allocation.

---

## When to Apply

- **Strategic planning:** When defining or refreshing organizational strategy, the ToC provides the logical backbone for program portfolio decisions.
- **Program design:** When designing new programs or evaluating existing ones, the ToC makes explicit what must be true for the program to work.
- **Grant writing and fundraising:** Funders increasingly require a credible ToC to understand how their investment connects to impact.
- **Impact evaluation and learning:** The ToC defines what to measure and why — it is the measurement framework's starting point.
- **Partnership and coalition work:** When multiple organizations are working toward shared outcomes, an agreed ToC creates alignment on causal logic and role division.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- If the organization cannot articulate why its activities produce its intended outcomes (not just what the outcomes are), the ToC is missing or implicit — surface and stress-test it.
- A ToC that cannot be falsified is not a theory — it is a narrative. Include assumptions that, if untrue, would mean the approach fails.
- Long causal chains increase risk: each link in the pathway is an assumption. Shorten the chain where possible, or identify which assumptions are most critical to test first.
- The best ToCs are co-created with the communities being served — assumptions about what people need, want, or will do are most accurate when those people are in the room.
- Distinguish between the program theory (does the program work as designed?) and the impact theory (does the design address the root cause?). Both must hold.
- A ToC should be a live document, updated as evidence and context change — not a static grant deliverable filed and forgotten.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Activity logic, not change logic:** Describing what the organization does (training sessions, meals served, advocacy campaigns) without explaining the causal pathway from activity to behavior change to systemic impact.
- **Assuming that outputs equal outcomes:** Assuming that completing the activity (training 100 people) automatically produces the desired change (those people getting jobs and staying employed). Outputs are necessary but not sufficient.
- **Overly linear models in complex systems:** Social change rarely follows a simple linear chain. ToCs for systemic change work must account for feedback loops, contextual factors, and non-linear dynamics.
- **ToC by leadership, for leadership:** Theories of change developed by executive teams or consultants without input from frontline staff or the communities being served are often disconnected from on-the-ground realities.
- **Untestable assumptions:** Vague causal claims ("participants will be empowered to...") that cannot be measured or observed make the ToC useless as an evaluation or learning tool.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Theory of Change (Nonprofit)]
Definition: An explicit, testable map of how and why specific activities, for a specific population, are expected to produce long-term outcomes. Articulates the causal pathway from inputs to impact and makes assumptions at each link visible and testable.
Key distinctions:
• ToC ≠ logic model: ToC explains WHY the pathway works, not just WHAT is done
• Outputs (activities completed) ≠ outcomes (changes in people, systems, or conditions)
• A valid ToC must be falsifiable — guaranteed outcomes are not theories
• ToC should be co-developed with community members, not solely by leadership
When relevant: Strategic planning, program design, grant writing, impact evaluation, or multi-organization partnerships requiring shared causal logic.
Action guidance: Surface the ToC before evaluating a program. Map it if implicit. Identify the 2–3 critical assumptions that must hold and prioritize evidence on those.
```
