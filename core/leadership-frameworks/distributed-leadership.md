## Definition

Distributed Leadership is a perspective that views leadership as a practice distributed across multiple people and situations rather than concentrated in a single formal leader. Leadership emerges from the interactions, tools, and structures within a system, not just from the actions of individuals in positional authority.

Key principles:
- Leadership is a product of interactions among leaders, followers, and the situation.
- Multiple individuals can and do perform leadership functions at different times.
- Formal position is only one source of leadership influence.
- The focus shifts from "who is the leader?" to "how is leadership practiced here?"

This framework is particularly relevant in knowledge work, complex environments, and organizations that need agility and broad ownership.

---

## When to Apply

- **Complex problem solving:** When no single person has all the knowledge required to make good decisions.
- **Cross-functional or matrix environments:** When work requires coordination across boundaries and formal hierarchy is insufficient.
- **Innovation and learning organizations:** When the organization needs rapid adaptation and diverse perspectives.
- **Team autonomy initiatives:** When attempting to push decision-making closer to the work.
- **Scaling organizations:** When a single leader or small group can no longer effectively lead all aspects of the work.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- If a decision requires deep contextual or specialized knowledge that the formal leader lacks, push the decision to those who have the relevant expertise and stake.
- Leadership functions (direction-setting, alignment, commitment) can and should be performed by different people at different times.
- Formal leaders should focus on creating the conditions for distributed leadership (clarity of purpose, decision rights, psychological safety) rather than trying to lead everything themselves.
- When multiple people are exercising leadership, alignment mechanisms (shared goals, regular synchronization, clear boundaries) become more important, not less.
- Distributed leadership does not mean "no one is accountable" — it means accountability is distributed appropriately along with authority.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Distributed in name only:** Claiming leadership is distributed while still requiring all important decisions to flow through one person or a small group.
- **Chaos disguised as distribution:** Removing hierarchy without creating clear decision rights, coordination mechanisms, or accountability structures.
- **Heroic leader plus delegation theater:** The senior leader still makes all the real decisions but pretends others are empowered.
- **Ignoring power dynamics:** Assuming that simply declaring leadership is distributed will overcome existing status differences, expertise hierarchies, or political realities.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Distributed Leadership]
Definition: View of leadership as a practice spread across multiple actors and the situation, rather than residing only in formal positions. Leadership emerges from interactions, tools, and structures within the system.
Key distinctions:
• Distributed leadership is about how leadership work gets done, not just who holds titles
• It does not eliminate the need for formal leadership or accountability
• Effective distribution requires clear purpose, decision rights, and coordination mechanisms
• Knowledge work and complex environments benefit most from this approach
When relevant: Cross-functional initiatives, innovation efforts, scaling teams, or situations where no single person has sufficient knowledge or context.
Action guidance: Look for where leadership functions are actually being performed (not just who has the title). Strengthen the conditions that allow capable people to lead in their domains (clarity, safety, decision rights). Avoid both over-centralization and unstructured "everyone leads" chaos.
```
