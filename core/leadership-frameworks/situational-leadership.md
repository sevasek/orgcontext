## Definition

Situational Leadership is a practical leadership model that says there is no single best leadership style. Instead, the most effective style depends on the **readiness** (competence + commitment) of the individual or team being led.

The model defines four leadership styles matched to four follower readiness levels (R1–R4):

- **Directing (S1)** — High task focus, low relationship focus. Used when the follower has low competence and low commitment.
- **Coaching (S2)** — High task focus, high relationship focus. Used when the follower has some competence but low commitment.
- **Supporting (S3)** — Low task focus, high relationship focus. Used when the follower has high competence but variable commitment.
- **Delegating (S4)** — Low task focus, low relationship focus. Used when the follower has high competence and high commitment.

The leader’s job is to diagnose readiness and flexibly adjust their style — moving from directing to delegating as the person or team develops.

---

## When to Apply

- **Onboarding or developing new team members:** When people are learning new skills and need clear direction.
- **Skill-building or performance improvement:** When individuals have the ability but lack confidence or motivation.
- **Delegation decisions:** When deciding how much autonomy to give someone on a task.
- **Team composition changes:** When a team gains or loses experienced members.
- **Leadership transitions:** When a new manager needs a structured way to adapt to their team.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- Before choosing a leadership approach, assess the person’s **competence** (skill/knowledge) and **commitment** (motivation/confidence) for *this specific task*.
- If the follower is at low readiness (R1), use a Directing style — they need clear instructions and close supervision.
- If the follower is at high readiness (R4), use a Delegating style — they need autonomy and minimal oversight.
- When someone is improving, gradually shift from Coaching → Supporting → Delegating. Do not jump straight to delegation.
- Re-assess readiness regularly — people can move backward (e.g., after a setback) as well as forward.
- A leader who uses the same style with everyone is almost certainly under- or over-leading some team members.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **One-size-fits-all leadership:** Applying the same style (e.g., always delegating or always directing) regardless of readiness — the most common failure mode.
- **Delegating too early:** Giving high autonomy to someone who still needs direction and support, leading to frustration or failure.
- **Micromanaging high-readiness people:** Continuing to Direct or Coach experienced, motivated team members, which demotivates them.
- **Confusing Situational Leadership with lack of standards:** Using the model as an excuse to avoid accountability or hard conversations.
- **Static diagnosis:** Assessing readiness once and never revisiting it as the person grows or the task changes.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Situational Leadership]
Definition: A leadership model that says the best style depends on the readiness (competence + commitment) of the follower. Four styles: Directing, Coaching, Supporting, Delegating.
Key distinctions:
• Leadership style must match follower readiness — no universal “best” style
• Readiness is task-specific and can change over time
• Goal is to develop people toward high competence and high commitment (Delegating)
When relevant: Onboarding, skill development, delegation decisions, performance coaching, or team composition changes.
Action guidance: Before leading or delegating, diagnose the person’s readiness for the specific task. Adjust your style accordingly: Direct when low readiness, gradually move toward Delegate as readiness grows. Re-assess regularly and avoid using the same style with everyone.
```
