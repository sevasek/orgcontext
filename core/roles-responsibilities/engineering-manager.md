## Definition

An Engineering Manager (EM) is a people leader responsible for the health, growth, and effectiveness of an engineering team while ensuring delivery of high-quality technical work. The EM bridges technical execution and human systems.

Key responsibilities:
- **People development:** Coaching, career growth, performance management, and psychological safety for direct reports.
- **Team effectiveness:** Building processes, removing blockers, and fostering collaboration.
- **Delivery ownership:** Ensuring the team delivers valuable outcomes on time while maintaining technical quality.
- **Technical guidance:** Providing context on architecture and trade-offs without becoming a bottleneck for decisions.

The EM role is distinct from individual contributor (IC) technical leadership. The EM's primary output is the team's output and health, not personal code contributions.

---

## When to Apply

- **Team scaling or reorgs:** When hiring, onboarding, or restructuring teams to maintain velocity and morale.
- **Career conversations:** During 1:1s, performance reviews, or when engineers express interest in promotion or new challenges.
- **Delivery risks:** When technical projects are at risk due to team dynamics, skill gaps, or burnout.
- **Cross-team coordination:** When engineering teams must align with product, design, or other functions on priorities and trade-offs.
- **Conflict or low morale:** When interpersonal issues or unclear expectations are affecting productivity.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- If a decision primarily affects team health, growth, or long-term capability, the Engineering Manager owns it (e.g., hiring, performance, process).
- When technical direction conflicts with team capacity or skill level, the EM must surface the risk and propose alternatives rather than silently accepting overload.
- The EM should spend more time on 1:1s and coaching than on code reviews once the team exceeds ~5-6 people; direct technical involvement should decrease as team size grows.
- If an engineer is consistently blocked by the same class of problem (e.g., unclear requirements), the EM owns fixing the systemic issue, not just the individual instance.
- Promotion or compensation decisions must be based on observable impact and growth, not tenure or likability.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **EM as senior IC:** The manager who spends most of their time coding and treats management as a side job creates dependency and neglects people development.
- **EM as pure people manager with no technical context:** When the EM cannot understand technical trade-offs, they cannot effectively advocate for the team or make informed prioritization decisions.
- **Hero culture enabler:** An EM who rewards constant firefighting and overtime instead of sustainable delivery and process improvement.
- **Micromanagement of implementation:** The EM who dictates how engineers should write code instead of focusing on outcomes and removing obstacles.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Engineering Manager]
Definition: People leader accountable for the health, growth, and delivery effectiveness of an engineering team. Owns coaching, process, hiring, and team dynamics while ensuring high-quality technical outcomes.
Key distinctions:
• EM owns team health and capability (not just shipping code)
• EM is distinct from Tech Lead (who owns technical direction on projects)
• EM's leverage comes from developing others, not personal output
• EM must balance delivery pressure with long-term team sustainability
When relevant: Team performance issues, career growth conversations, hiring, reorgs, delivery risks, or cross-functional misalignment.
Action guidance: Route people, process, and team capacity questions to the Engineering Manager. Distinguish clearly from Product Manager (what to build) and Tech Lead (how to build it technically). If no dedicated EM exists, teams often suffer from unclear career paths, burnout, or inconsistent processes.
```
