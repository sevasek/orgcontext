## Definition

Belonging is the experience of being accepted, valued, and able to show up authentically within a group or organization without having to hide or minimize important aspects of one's identity. It goes beyond inclusion (being invited in) to the deeper sense that one truly fits and matters.

Belonging is not the same as fitting in. Fitting in often requires people to conform or suppress parts of themselves. True belonging allows people to contribute their full selves while still being connected to the group.

Research consistently links strong belonging to higher engagement, lower turnover, better collaboration, and greater willingness to take interpersonal risks (a key ingredient of psychological safety).

---

## When to Apply

- **Onboarding and team formation:** When new people join or teams are forming and need to build connection quickly.
- **Diversity and inclusion efforts:** When the organization has diverse talent but people from underrepresented groups report feeling like outsiders.
- **Major change or disruption:** When events (reorgs, pivots, layoffs, growth) threaten people's sense of place and identity.
- **Conflict or exclusion dynamics:** When subgroups are forming or certain voices are consistently marginalized.
- **Remote or hybrid work:** When physical distance makes organic belonging harder to develop.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- If people are censoring themselves or code-switching heavily, belonging is likely low, even if surface-level diversity metrics look good.
- Belonging is created through repeated small signals of acceptance and value, not through one-off events or statements.
- When someone raises an issue of exclusion or microaggression, treat it as data about the system rather than an attack on the group or individuals.
- Leaders have disproportionate impact on belonging — their behavior signals what is truly valued far more than official policies.
- Belonging and high standards are not in tension. The highest-performing teams often have both strong belonging and rigorous expectations.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Assimilation disguised as belonging:** Expecting everyone to adopt the dominant culture's norms and communication style in order to "fit in."
- **Tokenism:** Bringing in diverse people but not changing the underlying culture, processes, or power structures that determine who really belongs.
- **Belonging only for the majority:** Creating strong in-group belonging that actively or passively excludes those who are different.
- **Politeness over honesty:** Suppressing difficult conversations in the name of "keeping the peace," which actually erodes real belonging for those who feel unseen.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Belonging]
Definition: The felt experience of being accepted, valued, and able to contribute authentically without hiding important parts of one's identity. Distinct from mere inclusion (being present) or fitting in (conforming).
Key distinctions:
• Belonging is about feeling one truly matters and can show up as themselves
• Low belonging leads to self-censorship, disengagement, and higher turnover
• Belonging is built through consistent signals of acceptance, not one-off initiatives
• Strong belonging enables the risk-taking required for high performance and innovation
When relevant: Onboarding, diversity efforts, reorgs, remote/hybrid work, or when certain groups report feeling like outsiders.
Action guidance: Pay attention to who speaks up, who stays silent, and who leaves. Look for patterns of exclusion or assimilation pressure. Leaders set the tone through their own behavior and what they reward or tolerate. If belonging is low, psychological safety and honest feedback will also suffer.
```
