## Definition

Psychological safety is the shared belief among team members that it is safe to take interpersonal risks — to speak up, ask questions, admit mistakes, or challenge the status quo — without fear of punishment, embarrassment, or retaliation.

**It is a group-level property**, not an individual personality trait. It describes the team climate, not individual confidence. A high-performing person may be psychologically safe in one team and unsafe in another.

Google's Project Aristotle research (2016) identified psychological safety as the single most important predictor of team effectiveness across over 180 teams studied. High psychological safety correlates with learning behavior, innovation, error reporting, and retention.

Psychological safety is **not** the same as comfort or niceness. High-psychological-safety teams still debate vigorously, give hard feedback, and hold each other accountable. The difference is that these actions are motivated by care for the work and team — and are received without defensive fear.

---

## When to Apply

- **Leadership transitions or new team formation:** Safety must be actively rebuilt when team composition or leadership changes.
- **Team health assessment:** When diagnosing why a team isn't sharing bad news, isn't experimenting, or produces groupthink.
- **Post-mortems and retrospectives:** Psychological safety determines whether these processes produce genuine learning or surface-level blame avoidance.
- **Innovation initiatives:** New ideas require willingness to look foolish. Low psychological safety kills ideation before it starts.
- **Onboarding and inclusion:** New team members and underrepresented voices are most sensitive to cues of (un)safety. Explicit attention is needed.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- If a team never surfaces bad news until it becomes a crisis, assume low psychological safety — not high competence.
- When a leader responds to mistakes with visible frustration or blame (even once), they may set back months of safety-building. Model the behavior: share your own mistakes openly.
- In meetings, if the same 20% of people speak 80% of the time, this is a psychological safety signal, not just a facilitation issue.
- Before running a retrospective or post-mortem, assess whether participants believe the outcome will be used to blame or improve. If blame is likely, safety must be explicitly addressed first.
- If you want to know if a team feels safe, ask: "What's something you've been hesitant to bring up?" The answer tells you everything.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Comfort culture:** A team that is always agreeable and avoids hard conversations has low psychological safety hidden behind a niceness veneer. Real safety makes productive disagreement easier, not absent.
- **Individual confidence vs. team safety:** A team member who is confident and outspoken individually does not indicate team-level psychological safety. The question is whether all team members feel safe, not just the dominant voices.
- **Assumed safety:** Leaders often assume their team feels safe because no one has complained. People in unsafe climates rarely complain — they leave, or quietly disengage. Proactive measurement (surveys, 1:1s) is required.
- **Safety as excuse for low standards:** High psychological safety teams still hold high performance standards. Edmondson's research shows the highest-performing teams have *both* high psychological safety and high accountability. Only safety → low performance. Only accountability → anxiety and burnout.
