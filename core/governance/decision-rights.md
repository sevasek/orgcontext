## Definition

Decision Rights define **who has the authority to make which decisions** in an organization or team. They clarify the difference between input, recommendation, and final authority.

Clear decision rights answer the question: “Who gets to decide this?” They reduce politics, speed up execution, prevent decision bottlenecks, and increase accountability. Unlike RACI (which assigns roles to tasks), Decision Rights focus specifically on authority allocation.

Good decision rights frameworks specify:
- Which decisions are **strategic** vs **operational** vs **tactical**
- Who has **final authority** (often called the “decider” or “D”)
- Who must be **consulted** or **informed**
- How disagreements or escalations are resolved

High-performing organizations make decision rights explicit, document them, and revisit them when team structure or strategy changes.

---

## When to Apply

- **Organizational restructuring or scaling:** As teams grow, unclear authority causes paralysis or turf wars.
- **Slow decision-making:** When too many people feel they need to approve everything.
- **Cross-functional initiatives:** To prevent “decision by committee” or endless alignment loops.
- **Post-mortem on failed projects:** Often reveals decision rights were ambiguous or ignored.
- **New leader or team onboarding:** Clarify authority early to set expectations.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- The best decision rights give authority to the person closest to the information and with the highest accountability for outcomes.
- If a decision is being delayed or constantly escalated, your decision rights are probably unclear or misassigned.
- Push decisions down to the lowest competent level (default rule). Only escalate when truly necessary.
- Document major recurring decisions (strategic, financial, people, technical) rather than trying to cover every possible choice.
- Revisit decision rights after major changes in team size, strategy, or structure.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Decision by committee:** Everyone has a say, no one has final authority. Leads to slow, lowest-common-denominator decisions.
- **Phantom authority:** Someone is told they “own” a decision but lacks real power or resources to execute it.
- **Seagull management:** Leaders who delegate authority but then overrule decisions at the last minute, destroying trust.
- **Over-centralization:** All decisions flow to the top, creating bottlenecks and disempowering teams.
- **Unspoken hierarchy:** Official decision rights exist on paper, but real power is exercised through politics or informal influence.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Decision Rights]
Definition: Clear allocation of authority — who is allowed to make which decisions in the organization or team. They reduce politics, speed up execution, and increase accountability.
Key principles:
• Authority should sit with the person closest to the information who also carries accountability for the outcome
• Distinguish between strategic, operational, and tactical decisions
• Explicit decision rights are far better than implicit or political ones
• RACI is a practical tool to turn decision rights into specific assignments
When relevant: Slow or contested decisions, scaling teams, cross-functional work, restructuring, or post-mortems.
Action guidance: When a decision is stuck, immediately ask “Who has the decision right here?” If unclear or contested, define or clarify it. Push decisions down to the lowest competent level unless escalation is truly required. Revisit decision rights when team structure or strategy changes.
```
