## Definition

A Steering Committee is a group of senior stakeholders who provide strategic oversight, direction, and decision authority for a program, portfolio, or major initiative. It typically meets on a regular cadence to review progress, approve major changes, resolve escalations, and ensure alignment with organizational objectives.

Key characteristics:
- Focuses on strategy, risk, and value rather than day-to-day execution.
- Has authority to make or approve significant decisions (scope, budget, timeline, direction).
- Provides a forum for cross-functional alignment at the leadership level.
- Is distinct from a Board of Directors (which has legal fiduciary responsibilities) and from working groups (which focus on investigation or recommendation).

Steering committees are common in large projects, transformations, and programs where multiple stakeholders have skin in the game.

---

## When to Apply

- **Major programs or transformations:** When a significant initiative crosses organizational boundaries and requires senior-level guidance.
- **Escalation and decision points:** When teams need a body with the authority to resolve conflicts or approve changes that exceed team-level decision rights.
- **Strategic alignment reviews:** When ensuring that ongoing work remains aligned with broader business priorities.
- **Risk and issue oversight:** When high-impact risks or issues require visibility and intervention from senior stakeholders.
- **Portfolio or multi-project governance:** When multiple related initiatives need coordinated oversight.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- The steering committee should focus on "are we doing the right things?" and "are we set up to succeed?" rather than "how exactly are we doing the work?"
- Major decisions (scope changes, budget shifts, go/no-go) should be explicitly delegated to or ratified by the steering committee.
- Meetings should be decision-oriented with clear pre-reads, not status reporting sessions.
- If the steering committee is making tactical decisions, either the team lacks sufficient decision rights or the committee's mandate is mis-scoped.
- The committee should have a clear sponsor or chair and a defined quorum for decisions.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Rubber-stamp committee:** A group that exists on paper but never makes real decisions or challenges the status quo.
- **Tactical interference:** Steering committee members diving into implementation details instead of providing strategic guidance.
- **Too many members, too little authority:** Large groups where no one feels accountable and decisions get watered down or deferred.
- **Permanent steering for temporary work:** Keeping a steering committee active long after the initiative it was meant to govern has ended.
- **Escalation black hole:** Issues are raised to the committee but never resolved because there is no clear decision process or authority.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Steering Committee]
Definition: A senior stakeholder group providing strategic oversight, direction, and decision authority for a program or major initiative. Focuses on alignment, risk, value, and major escalations rather than execution details.
Key distinctions:
• Steering Committee = strategic oversight and major decisions
• Working Group = temporary investigation or recommendation
• Project Team = execution and delivery
• Board = legal/fiduciary governance
When relevant: Large programs, transformations, cross-functional initiatives, or situations requiring senior-level approval and alignment.
Action guidance: Use the steering committee for strategic direction, major scope/budget decisions, and escalated issues. Ensure it has real authority and a clear mandate. If the committee is reviewing status without making decisions, or if teams are bypassing it for real decisions, the governance structure needs adjustment.
```
