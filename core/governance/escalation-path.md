## Definition

An Escalation Path is a predefined, agreed-upon process for resolving decisions, conflicts, or blockers that cannot be handled at the current level of authority. It clearly defines **who gets involved next** and **under what conditions** when a decision is stalled, decision rights are unclear/contested, or agreement cannot be reached.

A good escalation path:
- Prevents endless loops and decision paralysis
- Protects psychological safety by making escalation a normal, non-personal process
- Maintains accountability while providing a safety valve
- Specifies timing (e.g., “escalate after 48 hours of no resolution”)
- Identifies the final decision-maker at each level

Escalation paths are a critical companion to Decision Rights and RACI — they activate when those frameworks are unclear, contested, or insufficient.

---

## When to Apply

- **Cross-functional or high-stakes projects:** When multiple teams have overlapping interests.
- **Decision bottlenecks:** When a decision is stuck despite clear initial ownership.
- **Conflict between peers or functions:** When two Accountable parties disagree.
- **Team charters and operating agreements:** Define escalation paths at the start of any new team or initiative.
- **Post-mortems:** Analyze whether missing or ignored escalation paths contributed to delays or poor outcomes.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- Escalate on **time** (e.g., after 48–72 hours of no progress) or **impact** (if the issue affects key goals, customers, or strategy).
- Always attempt resolution at the lowest possible level first — escalation should be the exception, not the default.
- The person escalating should document the issue, attempted solutions, and recommended options before handing it up.
- The escalated person must respond with a decision or clear next steps within an agreed timeframe.
- Leaders should treat escalation as valuable information about systemic issues (e.g., unclear decision rights), not as failure.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **No escalation path:** Issues bounce around indefinitely or die in silence.
- **Escalation as betrayal:** Treating someone who escalates as disloyal or weak. This destroys psychological safety.
- **Bypassing levels:** Jumping straight to the CEO instead of following the defined path. This undermines authority and creates chaos.
- **Escalation theater:** Constantly escalating minor issues to avoid accountability.
- **No follow-up:** Escalations go up but never receive a timely decision or feedback, breeding frustration and disengagement.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Escalation Path]
Definition: A predefined, agreed-upon process for resolving stalled decisions, conflicts, or blockers when they cannot be handled at the current level of authority. It clearly defines who gets involved next and under what conditions.
Key principles:
• Escalate on time (e.g. 48–72 hours) or business impact
• Always attempt resolution at the lowest competent level first
• Document the issue, attempted solutions, and options before escalating
• Escalation is normal governance — not betrayal or failure
• Works hand-in-hand with Decision Rights and RACI
When relevant: Decision bottlenecks, peer conflicts, cross-functional stalls, or high-stakes issues.
Action guidance: When a decision is stuck, follow the defined escalation path. Provide clear context and options. The receiving leader must respond promptly with a decision or next steps. Use repeated escalations as signals to improve underlying decision rights or RACI clarity.
```
