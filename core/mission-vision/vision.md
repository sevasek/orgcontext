## Definition

A **Vision** is a clear, compelling, and aspirational description of what an organization wants to become or achieve in the long term. It paints a vivid picture of a desired future state — typically 5–10+ years out — that inspires and guides people.

Unlike the **Mission** (why we exist and what we do today), the Vision is about **where we are going**. It serves as a North Star: motivational, directional, and emotionally resonant. A strong vision is specific enough to guide decisions but broad enough to allow creativity in how to achieve it.

Effective visions are vivid and imaginable, ambitious yet believable, emotionally compelling, and actively used as a filter for strategy and opportunities.

---

## When to Apply

- **Strategic planning and annual goal setting:** Use the vision to ensure initiatives move the organization toward the desired future.
- **Leadership communication:** When rallying teams, explaining change, or onboarding new members.
- **Evaluating opportunities:** Does this new project, product, or partnership bring us closer to our vision?
- **Culture and hiring:** Attracting and retaining people who are inspired by the same future state.
- **Times of uncertainty or change:** When short-term pressures threaten long-term direction.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- Ask of any major decision: “Does this move us closer to our vision, or pull us away from it?”
- A vision should make it easier to say “no” to good-but-off-track opportunities.
- Test your vision by asking: “If we achieved this, would we be proud? Would it matter?”
- Communicate the vision repeatedly and tie concrete work back to it — people need to see the connection.
- Revisit the vision every 3–5 years. It should evolve as the world and capabilities change, but not constantly.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Vision as slogan:** Vague, generic statements (“We want to be the best…”) that could apply to any company. No emotional pull or guidance.
- **Vision–Mission confusion:** Treating vision and mission as interchangeable, which weakens both.
- **Vision without execution:** A beautiful vision that is never translated into strategy, goals, or trade-off decisions.
- **Static vision:** Clinging to an outdated vision despite major market or technological shifts.
- **Leader-only vision:** A vision that lives only in the CEO’s head instead of being shared and owned by the organization.

[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Vision]
Definition: A clear, compelling, aspirational picture of the desired future state (typically 5–10+ years ahead). It answers "Where are we going?" and acts as a North Star for long-term direction and motivation.
Key distinctions:
• Vision = long-term aspiration (future state)
• Mission = why we exist and what we do today
• Vision inspires and guides; Mission focuses and filters daily decisions
When relevant: Strategic planning, evaluating new opportunities, leadership communication, culture building, or navigating uncertainty/change.
Action guidance: When assessing any initiative or decision, ask "Does this move us meaningfully closer to our vision?" Use the vision to say "no" to attractive but off-track opportunities. Regularly connect day-to-day work back to the vision so people understand the bigger picture they contribute to.
