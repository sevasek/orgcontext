## Definition

Organizational Purpose is the fundamental reason an organization exists beyond profit or survival — the contribution it seeks to make to its customers, employees, and society. It answers the question: "Why do we exist?"

Purpose is distinct from:
- **Mission**: What the organization does and for whom (more operational and current-state).
- **Vision**: Where the organization is heading (aspirational future state).
- **Values**: The principles that guide behavior.

A strong purpose provides an enduring anchor for decision-making during uncertainty, inspires employees, guides strategy, and helps the organization make trade-offs that are consistent over time. It is less about what the organization does and more about the impact it aims to create.

---

## When to Apply

- **Strategic decisions under uncertainty:** When choosing between options that have similar financial returns but different impacts on the organization's reason for being.
- **Culture and employee engagement:** When attracting talent or aligning people around something more meaningful than quarterly targets.
- **Brand and external positioning:** When communicating why the organization matters to customers, partners, and society.
- **Major pivots or transformations:** When the organization is redefining itself and needs a north star that goes beyond the current business model.
- **Ethical or values-based dilemmas:** When short-term pressures conflict with the organization's deeper reason for existing.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- If a decision feels like it "doesn't feel like us," test it against the stated purpose.
- Purpose should be stable enough to guide the organization through multiple strategy cycles but vivid enough to be actionable.
- Leaders should be able to explain how day-to-day work connects to the purpose — if they can't, the purpose is not yet embedded.
- When purpose and short-term financial performance appear in conflict, the purpose should at minimum force an explicit conversation about the trade-off.
- Purpose is most powerful when it is used as a filter for what the organization chooses *not* to do.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Purpose as marketing slogan:** A beautiful statement on the website that has no influence on strategy, hiring, product decisions, or resource allocation.
- **Confusing purpose with mission or vision:** Treating all three terms as interchangeable, which weakens the distinct role each plays in guiding the organization.
- **Purpose without accountability:** Leaders who talk about purpose but reward and promote based solely on financial or operational metrics.
- **Generic purpose:** Statements that could apply to almost any organization ("to make the world a better place") and therefore provide no real guidance.
- **Purpose that only lives at the top:** A purpose defined by the founder or CEO that is never socialized or owned by the broader organization.
