## Definition

A BHAG (Big Hairy Audacious Goal) is a long-term, inspiring, and challenging goal that serves as a unifying focal point for an organization's efforts over 10–30 years. Coined by James Collins and Jerry Porras in *Built to Last*, a BHAG is designed to be so bold that it requires the organization to stretch far beyond its current capabilities.

Characteristics of an effective BHAG:
- Clear, compelling, and easy to understand
- Audacious and risky — success is not guaranteed
- Long-term (typically 10+ years)
- Exciting and energizing to people inside and outside the organization
- Provides a sense of direction and cohesion without prescribing the exact path

Examples from history include landing a man on the moon, becoming the most respected car company in the world, or democratizing access to information.

---

## When to Apply

- **Defining or refreshing long-term vision:** When the organization needs a north star that goes beyond quarterly or even multi-year planning.
- **Major transformations or turnarounds:** When the company must make a significant leap to survive or thrive.
- **Inspiring and aligning large groups:** When the organization is large, distributed, or facing uncertainty and needs a shared, motivating ambition.
- **Attracting talent and partners:** When the company wants to draw in people and organizations who are excited by bold missions.
- **Strategic planning cycles:** During visioning exercises that look beyond typical 3–5 year horizons.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- A good BHAG should feel slightly uncomfortable or unrealistic to most people inside the organization at the time it is set.
- The BHAG should be so clear that it can guide decisions even when detailed strategy is unclear.
- BHAGs work best when they are combined with a vivid description of what success looks like, not just a slogan.
- The organization should be willing to make short-term sacrifices and bold bets in service of the BHAG.
- BHAGs are not annual goals or OKRs — they are meant to outlast multiple leadership teams and planning cycles.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **BHAG as marketing slogan:** A lofty-sounding statement that leadership does not actually use to make hard resource or strategic decisions.
- **Uninspiring or incremental goal:** Setting a goal that is merely an extension of current trajectory rather than a true stretch.
- **No vivid description:** Announcing a BHAG without painting a concrete picture of what the world (or the company) will look like when it is achieved.
- **Ignoring the "how":** Believing the BHAG alone is sufficient without also building the strategy, capabilities, and culture needed to pursue it.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: BHAG]
Definition: A Big Hairy Audacious Goal — a clear, bold, long-term (10–30 year) ambition that stretches the organization far beyond its current capabilities and serves as a unifying focal point.
Key distinctions:
• BHAGs are intentionally audacious and long-term (unlike OKRs or annual goals)
• A strong BHAG is clear enough to guide decisions even in uncertainty
• BHAGs must be paired with strategy and capability-building
• Effective BHAGs inspire both internally and externally
When relevant: Vision setting, major transformations, or when the organization needs a unifying long-term direction.
Action guidance: Use BHAGs for true 10+ year ambition. Ensure clarity and be willing to make difficult bets in service of it. If the stated BHAG does not influence major decisions, it is not functioning as a real BHAG.
```
