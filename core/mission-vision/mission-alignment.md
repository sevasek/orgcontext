## Definition

Mission alignment is the degree to which an organization's decisions, resource allocations, team behaviors, and priorities are consistent with its stated mission. A mission describes *why the organization exists* and *what it does* — distinct from vision (what it aspires to become) and values (how it behaves).

Alignment is not passive agreement with the mission statement. It is active, operational, and testable: decisions that advance the mission are "in alignment"; decisions that trade mission progress for short-term convenience are "misaligned."

In well-aligned organizations, the mission functions as a decision filter: "Does this choice advance our mission or not?" becomes a usable question, not a rhetorical one. Teams can explain *how* their work connects to the mission without prompting. Leaders reject opportunities that are financially attractive but mission-detracting.

---

## When to Apply

- **Prioritization and resource allocation:** Use mission alignment as a filter when multiple initiatives compete for limited resources.
- **Evaluating new opportunities:** A new product, partnership, or market may be profitable but misaligned with mission — mission alignment makes this tradeoff explicit.
- **Team goal-setting:** Team OKRs or quarterly plans should trace back to mission explicitly.
- **Conflict resolution:** When teams disagree on direction, the mission can serve as a neutral arbiter — "which option better advances our mission?"

---

## Decision Heuristics

- Ask of any major initiative: "If we do this and it succeeds, does our mission advance?" If no, that's a misalignment signal, not automatic rejection — but the tension should be named and decided consciously.
- If the mission statement can't be used to reject any initiative (i.e., everything passes), the mission is too broad to be a useful filter. It may need refinement.
- When resources are constrained and cuts are needed, mission-aligned work should be protected first.
- If different leaders give different answers to "what is our mission?", that is a leadership alignment problem with downstream consequences — not just a communications issue.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Mission statement theater:** Organizations with beautiful mission statements that don't inform real decisions. The test: can leaders explain the last time they said *no* to something because it wasn't mission-aligned?
- **Mission drift:** Gradual accumulation of work that is tangential to the mission, usually through "yes-and" culture or opportunity-chasing. Often only visible in retrospect when the organization has lost identity.
- **Mission vs. vision confusion:** Mission = why we exist and what we do today. Vision = what we're building toward. Treating them as interchangeable weakens both.
- **Alignment as conformity:** Mission alignment should create *decision clarity*, not *thought conformity*. Debate about *how* to advance the mission is healthy. Debate about *whether* to abandon it for convenience is different.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Mission Alignment]

Definition: The degree to which decisions, priorities, and behaviors are consistent with the organization's mission (why it exists and what it does). A decision-filter: "Does this advance our mission?"

When this is relevant:
- Prioritizing competing initiatives
- Evaluating new opportunities, partnerships, or products
- Diagnosing why teams feel unfocused or strategy feels scattered

Key distinctions:
- Mission = why we exist / what we do now
- Vision = what we're building toward
- Alignment ≠ blind agreement; it's an active decision filter

Apply this by: When evaluating a proposed initiative or decision, ask "how does this advance the mission?" If the answer is unclear or absent, surface the misalignment explicitly. If everything passes the mission filter, flag that the mission may be too broad to provide useful guidance.
```
