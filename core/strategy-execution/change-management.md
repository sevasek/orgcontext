## Definition

Change Management is the structured approach to transitioning individuals, teams, and organizations from a current state to a desired future state. It addresses the human, cultural, and process dimensions of change, not just the technical or structural aspects.

Effective change management recognizes that:
- People do not resist change itself as much as they resist the loss, uncertainty, and disruption it creates.
- Successful change requires both a compelling vision and a practical plan for adoption.
- Resistance is information — it often signals legitimate concerns or missing context.

Common frameworks include Kotter’s 8 Steps, ADKAR (Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement), and Lewin’s Unfreeze–Change–Refreeze model.

---

## When to Apply

- **Organizational transformations:** When implementing new strategies, structures, technologies, or ways of working at scale.
- **Process or tool adoptions:** When rolling out new methodologies (Agile, OKRs, etc.), platforms, or operating models.
- **Mergers, acquisitions, or reorgs:** When combining cultures, teams, or systems.
- **Strategic pivots:** When the organization must shift direction significantly and quickly.
- **Cultural evolution:** When attempting to change behaviors, norms, or values (e.g., increasing psychological safety or customer focus).

---

## Decision Heuristics

- If a change initiative is failing, first diagnose whether the problem is in the "what" (the change itself) or the "how" (how the change is being led and adopted).
- Never underestimate the power of loss aversion — people will fight to protect the status quo even when the current state is painful.
- The larger the change, the more deliberate communication and reinforcement are required. One announcement is never enough.
- Change that is done *to* people rather than *with* people generates resistance. Involvement increases ownership.
- If senior leaders are not visibly and consistently modeling the new behaviors, the change will not stick.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Change by decree:** Announcing a major change and expecting compliance without addressing why it matters, what it means for individuals, or how support will be provided.
- **Ignoring the middle:** Focusing only on executives and frontline staff while neglecting middle management, who are often the most critical (and resistant) layer.
- **Training as the only intervention:** Believing that teaching people new skills is sufficient without also addressing motivation, incentives, and environmental barriers.
- **Big bang without pilots:** Rolling out large-scale change everywhere at once instead of learning through smaller experiments.

[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Change Management]
Definition: Structured approach to moving people, teams, and organizations from current state to a desired future state. Addresses the human side of change (fear, loss, adoption) in addition to technical or structural changes.
Key distinctions:
• Change Management focuses on adoption and human transition, not just project delivery or new processes
• Resistance is often rational and contains useful information
• Successful change requires both vision (why) and practical support (how)
• Leaders must model the change, not just sponsor it
When relevant: Transformations, new methodology or tool rollouts, reorgs, strategy shifts, or cultural initiatives.
