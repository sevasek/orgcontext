## Definition

A **North Star Metric (NSM)** is a single, critical metric that best represents the core value your product or service delivers to customers and the long-term health of your business. It acts as a guiding "North Star" — one number that the entire organization can rally around and use to evaluate progress toward the mission and vision.

Unlike vanity metrics (e.g. total registered users) or output metrics (e.g. lines of code), a good North Star Metric is:
- **Value-focused**: Directly tied to the benefit customers receive
- **Actionable**: Influenced by the team's work
- **Measurable** over time
- **Leading indicator** of sustainable growth, retention, and long-term success

Examples:
- Airbnb: Nights Booked
- Facebook: Daily Active Users (in early days)
- Slack: Messages Sent per Active User
- Spotify: Time Spent Listening

---

## When to Apply

- **Strategic alignment:** When teams need a single focal point to align decisions across departments.
- **Product prioritization:** When choosing which features or initiatives to build next.
- **Growth strategy:** When defining what "success" looks like beyond revenue or short-term KPIs.
- **Company scaling:** When the organization grows and risks losing focus on what truly matters.
- **OKR setting:** As the primary lagging indicator that quarterly OKRs should support.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- Ask: “If this metric improves, are our customers receiving more of the core value we promise?”
- A true North Star Metric should be hard to “game” and resistant to short-term manipulation.
- If improving the metric doesn’t move the business forward in a meaningful way, it’s not the right North Star.
- The North Star should be reviewed annually but not changed frequently — stability matters.
- Every major team or department should be able to explain how their work influences the North Star Metric.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Vanity North Star:** Using total sign-ups or page views instead of a value metric (looks good but doesn’t reflect real usage or retention).
- **Too many North Stars:** Trying to track 5–10 “equally important” metrics. This defeats the purpose of having one guiding star.
- **Output instead of outcome:** Using deployment frequency or story points completed as the North Star instead of customer value delivered.
- **Short-term focus:** Choosing a metric that optimizes this quarter’s revenue at the expense of long-term customer health.
- **Ignoring leading indicators:** Relying solely on the North Star without supporting input/leading metrics (e.g. only tracking revenue without engagement or retention).

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: North Star Metric]
Definition: A single metric that best captures the core value delivered to customers and the long-term health/success of the business. It serves as the primary "North Star" for strategic alignment and decision-making.
Key principles:
• Focuses on customer value delivered (not vanity or output metrics)
• Must be actionable, measurable, and difficult to game
• Acts as a decision filter: "Does this initiative meaningfully improve our North Star Metric?"
When relevant: Strategic planning, product prioritization, growth initiatives, OKR setting, or when the organization feels unfocused/scattered.
Action guidance: When evaluating any initiative, feature, or trade-off, ask "How does this move our North Star Metric?" Use the North Star to say "no" to work that doesn't contribute to long-term value. Always support it with leading indicators and team-level metrics that influence it.
```
