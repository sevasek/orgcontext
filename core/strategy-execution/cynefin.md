## Definition

Cynefin (pronounced "kuh-NEV-in," Welsh for "habitat" or "place of belonging") is a sensemaking framework that helps leaders identify the nature of a situation and choose the appropriate response. It defines five domains:

- **Clear (formerly Simple):** Cause and effect are obvious. Best practices exist. Sense → Categorize → Respond. Example: expense reimbursement process.
- **Complicated:** Cause and effect exist but aren't immediately obvious. Expertise is needed to analyze. Sense → Analyze → Respond. Example: diagnosing a system performance issue.
- **Complex:** Cause and effect can only be understood *in retrospect*. No single right answer. Probe → Sense → Respond. Example: entering a new market, culture change.
- **Chaotic:** No cause-and-effect relationship is perceivable. Immediate action needed to restore order. Act → Sense → Respond. Example: system outage, crisis.
- **Confused (the center):** Not knowing which domain you're in. The goal is to move out of confusion by gathering information and deciding.

Misdiagnosing the domain — applying best practices to a complex problem, or treating a clear problem as complex — is one of the most common leadership failure modes.

---

## When to Apply

- **Problem triage:** Before deciding how to solve a problem, use Cynefin to determine what *kind* of problem it is.
- **Process design:** Clear problems warrant standardized processes; complex problems require experimentation and emergence.
- **Leadership approach selection:** The appropriate leadership style varies by domain — directive in chaos, expert-consulting in complicated, facilitative in complex.
- **Avoiding best-practice traps:** When a team is applying documented best practices to a problem and repeatedly failing, Cynefin suggests the problem may be complex, not complicated.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- Before applying a solution, ask: "Is this problem one where an expert can analyze it and give an answer (Complicated), or one where we must experiment and learn (Complex)?" Choose your approach accordingly.
- In crisis (Chaotic), act first to stabilize — don't over-analyze. Once stability is restored, shift to Complicated or Complex reasoning.
- If your team keeps producing plans that reality immediately invalidates, you may be treating a Complex problem as Complicated. Switch to short experiments and feedback loops.
- Clear domain problems should be systematized and delegated — leaders spending significant time here are misallocating attention.
- Be cautious of "obvious" answers in high-stakes situations. If everyone agrees immediately and no one sees any risk, you may be in the danger zone between Clear and Chaotic (complacency cliff).

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Complexity theater:** Calling a straightforward, well-understood problem "complex" to avoid accountability for outcomes. Some problems genuinely do have right answers.
- **Best-practice imposition on complex problems:** Applying Six Sigma, detailed project plans, or rigid processes to complex adaptive challenges. These tools reduce variance — useful in Complicated, harmful in Complex where variance is how you learn.
- **Staying in Chaotic:** Some leaders are comfortable in crisis mode and inadvertently keep problems chaotic by never building toward stability. Chaotic is a temporary state for response, not a management style.
- **Ignoring domain transitions:** Problems move between domains. A situation that was Complicated (pre-market entry) becomes Complex (in-market adaptation) and may temporarily go Chaotic (competitive disruption). Regular re-assessment is needed.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Cynefin Framework]

Definition: A sensemaking framework with five domains. Clear: best practices apply (sense → categorize → respond). Complicated: expert analysis needed (sense → analyze → respond). Complex: cause-effect only visible in retrospect, use safe-to-fail experiments (probe → sense → respond). Chaotic: act first to stabilize (act → sense → respond). Confused: don't know which domain yet.

When this is relevant:
- Deciding how to approach an unfamiliar problem
- Evaluating why a plan keeps failing despite good execution
- Choosing between expert-led vs. experiment-led approaches

Key distinctions:
- Complicated ≠ Complex: Complicated has a knowable answer; Complex does not yet.
- Best practices harm Complex problems — they suppress the variation needed for learning.

Apply this by: Classify the problem domain before recommending an approach. Complex → small safe-to-fail experiments. Chaotic → immediate stabilizing action. Complicated → bring in relevant expertise.
```
