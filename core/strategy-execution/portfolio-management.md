## Definition

Portfolio Management is the centralized management of one or more portfolios to achieve strategic objectives. It involves selecting, prioritizing, resourcing, and governing a collection of projects, programs, and initiatives to maximize value while managing risk and capacity.

Unlike project management (delivering a specific initiative well) or program management (coordinating related initiatives), portfolio management operates at the enterprise or business-unit level. It asks: "Are we working on the right things, in the right balance, with the right resources?"

Key activities include:
- Defining investment themes and strategic fit criteria
- Evaluating and selecting initiatives (often using scoring models, strategic alignment, and risk/return)
- Balancing the portfolio (e.g., short-term vs. long-term, high-risk vs. low-risk, run/grow/transform)
- Continuously re-prioritizing as strategy, capacity, and market conditions change
- Killing or pausing initiatives that no longer justify their investment

---

## When to Apply

- **Resource contention:** When there are more good ideas than available people, money, or time.
- **Strategic alignment reviews:** When the organization needs to ensure that current work actually advances stated strategy.
- **Annual or quarterly planning:** During budgeting and roadmap cycles where investment decisions are made.
- **Mergers, acquisitions, or major pivots:** When the set of active initiatives must be re-evaluated against new priorities.
- **Chronic overload:** When teams are constantly context-switching or missing commitments due to too many concurrent efforts.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- Every initiative in the portfolio should have a clear strategic rationale; "it seemed like a good idea" is not sufficient justification.
- The portfolio should be actively managed — regular reviews that result in stopping or deprioritizing work are a sign of healthy portfolio management, not failure.
- Capacity is the ultimate constraint. Adding more projects without removing others simply increases thrashing and reduces overall throughput.
- High strategic alignment on paper can mask poor portfolio balance. A portfolio full of "strategic" projects that are all high-risk or all long-term may still be dangerous.
- The cost of continuing a marginal initiative is not just its direct spend — it is the opportunity cost of the capacity it consumes.

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Project hoarding:** Keeping every initiative alive because no one wants to be the one who "killed" it, leading to a bloated, under-resourced portfolio.
- **Pet project protection:** Senior leaders shielding their favorite initiatives from portfolio scrutiny or prioritization processes.
- **Scorecard theater:** Using elaborate scoring models that create an illusion of objectivity while the real decisions are still made politically.
- **Treating portfolio management as a one-time event:** Doing a big prioritization exercise once a year and then letting the portfolio drift for the next 11 months.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Portfolio Management]
Definition: Centralized selection, prioritization, resourcing, and governance of a collection of initiatives to maximize strategic value while managing risk and capacity. Operates above the level of individual projects or programs.
Key distinctions:
• Portfolio management decides which initiatives to fund and continue (strategic selection)
• Project management delivers a specific initiative well
• Program management coordinates related initiatives
• Healthy portfolio management requires regularly stopping work, not just starting it
When relevant: Resource contention, annual/quarterly planning, strategy alignment reviews, or when the organization has more ideas than capacity.
Action guidance: Maintain a living portfolio view. Use clear criteria for selection and continuation. Actively kill or pause initiatives that no longer justify resources. Watch for pet projects and capacity overload. If everything is "strategic," nothing is.
```
