## Definition

A **Strategy Map** is a visual diagram that shows how an organization creates value by linking strategic objectives across multiple perspectives. It translates high-level strategy into a clear, cause-and-effect logic model.

The classic Strategy Map (from Kaplan & Norton’s Balanced Scorecard) uses four perspectives:
- **Financial** — What financial outcomes do we want to achieve?
- **Customer** — How will we create value for our customers?
- **Internal Processes** — What processes must we excel at?
- **Learning & Growth** — What capabilities, culture, and people do we need?

The map shows **cause-and-effect relationships** (e.g., better employee skills → improved processes → higher customer satisfaction → better financial results). It makes strategy visible, memorable, and actionable.

A good Strategy Map answers: “How do we create value?” and serves as a communication and alignment tool.

---

## When to Apply

- **Strategy development or refresh:** When turning a mission/vision into concrete, connected objectives.
- **Organizational alignment:** When different departments or teams need to see how their work contributes to overall success.
- **Communication to the organization:** When leaders need a single visual to explain strategy to employees.
- **OKR and goal cascading:** As a foundation for creating aligned OKRs across the company.
- **Performance reviews and course correction:** To check whether current initiatives support the strategic logic.

---

## Decision Heuristics

- Every objective on the map should have a clear “if we improve X, then Y improves” cause-and-effect link.
- If a proposed initiative doesn’t connect to any objective on the Strategy Map, it should be challenged or deprioritized.
- The map should have 15–25 objectives maximum — more than that usually means it’s trying to do too much.
- Review the map at least annually. Strategy Maps should evolve as the environment and capabilities change.
- Test the map with employees: Can they explain in their own words how their daily work supports the map?

---

## Counter-Examples / Anti-Patterns

- **Strategy Map as decoration:** A beautiful diagram that is created once and never referenced again.
- **Too many objectives:** Crowded maps with 40+ items that become overwhelming rather than clarifying.
- **Missing cause-and-effect links:** A list of objectives without showing *how* they connect (this is just a goals list, not a Strategy Map).
- **Financial-only focus:** Maps that ignore Learning & Growth and Internal Processes — leading to short-term thinking.
- **Static map in a dynamic world:** Refusing to update the map despite major market, technology, or mission shifts.

## Prompt Snippet

```
[ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT: Strategy Map]
Definition: A visual cause-and-effect diagram that shows how an organization creates value by linking objectives across perspectives (typically Financial, Customer, Internal Processes, Learning & Growth). It translates strategy into a coherent story.
Key principles:
• Shows clear cause-and-effect relationships between objectives
• Bridges high-level vision/mission with day-to-day execution
• Serves as a communication and alignment tool
When relevant: Strategic planning, OKR creation, cross-team alignment, or when evaluating whether initiatives support the overall strategy.
Action guidance: When reviewing a new initiative or goal, ask "Where does this fit on our Strategy Map?" and "What cause-and-effect link does it strengthen?" Use the Strategy Map to maintain coherence and say "no" to work that does not meaningfully advance the strategic logic.
```
